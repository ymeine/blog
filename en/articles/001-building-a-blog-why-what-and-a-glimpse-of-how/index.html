Why a blog?

It’s been a while I had the idea of creating a blog. I didn’t note it down, but I would say roughly 6-7 years, around that.

I’ve been doing software development for 16 years now, including 5 years at school and therefore 11 years as a professional developer. And during that whole time I constantly watched the state of the art, I learned, experimented, tested things, created, reinvented the wheel many times, tested and switched technologies, and so on and so forth.

I actually have tens of current projects, and had a bunch more by the past. Some I kind of dropped, some that stayed and evolved purely on my mind, some I coded partially, and often rewrote from scratch 2 or 3 times. It’s actually hard to both develop projects and keep up with the technologies when having very little spare time. Plus, I may talk about how I used to work in a future blog post, but I was never doing things by the book… It was probably good for learning how to get crafty, but not so much for projects maintenance and durability: I could never enjoy the full potential of existing tools.

The reasons

The first reason why I’m creating a blog: I now have more free time — actually I’m full time on my projects. So to me an additional blog project is ok to fit in. I’ll probably dedicate an article to listing all the projects I work on or have on my roadmap, plus those I worked on by the past.

The second reason is that, despite all those years I spent practicing SO MUCH, I could not really achieve anything I consider ready for sharing with the world: libraries, applications, tools. I actually have very high quality expectations regarding what I create — maybe too high sometimes, that’s something I’m still working on. Also, as I said above, spare time was short, technologies evolve fast, and I had to make a choice: favor learning or achievement. I chose the former.

However, what I can start sharing from now on is knowledge, experience. It’s just a matter of writing nice articles, which takes time, but has much less constraints than sharing code, programs or else.

Another reason, summarized by the quote I saw on that blog:

I hear and I forget. I see and I remember. I write and I understand.

I really like that quote since I always understood much more when writing documentation or explaining things to people, and sharing content in a blog is similar, just a bit more fun.

What you will find in here

Well, as you could guess: topics related to software development. But which ones in particular? That’s the thing: I don’t set myself any limit. I want this blog to be generalist, but still dive deep in details when I want.

Given my experience, expect more content regarding web development in every sense, and maybe more on the side of frontend. But I’ll also share about systems development, automation, methodology/organization, maybe hardware, and more.

I will also add some topics related to my other passion: music making. Stay tuned

How I started creating this blog

The requirements

A blog has specific requirements, and I identify these ones as the top priorities:

  • be performant: it’s mostly pure reading material, people won’t forgive a slow load or a laggy scroll, both on desktop or mobile
  • be SEO/metadata friendly: what would be the purpose if people can’t find or share the blog properly?
  • be appealing: I need to make sure that a user wants to stay, and for that I need full control over the design and features of my blog

Now, I have my own requirements as well, and that is:

  • use DX-friendly technologies, matching my skills and my desire of control
  • have a purely static website (I’ll explain that below)

Picking the right tool

For performance and SEO/metadata, which are topics I’m not 100% comfortable with, I first thought about using a CMS, WordPress being at the top of the list. But that wouldn’t have matched the “DX-friendly” and “purely static website” requirements. WordPress is PHP based, requires a full backend stack, and allows UI control mostly through customization, not from the ground up (AFAIK).

What I really feel comfortable with is creating content using frameworks or libraries that I usually use for applications development, but which are fully capable or making websites. So in that field of modern tools, things like Hugo, Gatsby or Astro came out.

Tools in the category of Hugo or Jekyll use specific or marginal templating languages and conventions, which means that if I ever wanna change the tool, I will have to rewrite much more code compared to if I pick one of the options I present next.

Gatsby and Astro on the other hand belong to another category: they mix static site generation features with famous components libraries like React, Svelte, Solid, which were initially used more for full client-side SPA development. Next.js is another option, but when I picked my choice I think it was still not ready for static site export in version 13.

Gatsby didn’t feel so appealing regarding DX. It is also locking to React. Don’t get me wrong, I love React — for the good and the bad — but when I saw that Astro looked dead simple, progressive and could support most of the modern component libraries — including React, Preact, Svelte, Solid, Vue, Qwik — I didn’t hesitate.

At least at first.

I’ll share more details about the process, the technical aspects, the pros and cons of Astro and the researches I made to see if other tools would fill its gaps. But that will be for a future blog post.

What do I mean by “purely static website”?

All websites and web-based applications can potentially work without a backend. In that case, the server only hosts and serves what we refer to as “static files”, without interpreting them. The whole execution process happens exclusively on the client side. Such files are HTML, CSS or JavaScript, as well as potentially other assets like images.

Getting rid of a “dynamic” backend has a big advantage: it opens the door to more hosting solutions, which are usually free. GitHub pages, which is hosting that blog right now, is a perfect choice since I already use GitHub to handle my projects. And I even went onboard with their comments system Giscus. It’s all consistent.

However here I implicitly added another meaning to the denomination “purely static website”: I needed a tool that can render the whole — or most of the — website content at build time, instead of having an almost empty, single HTML page which would be filled by JavaScript on the client side. With that, we tick two important requirements:

  • SEO/metadata: since the content is directly on the server
  • performance: since the browser will quickly paint what it receives, before executing any JavaScript code

What’s next?

As I said earlier, I’ll share more details about the blog creation process in a future post. It’s already listed in the roadmap.

Why not now? One could argue this is completely in sync with this article’s topic.

I choose not now because I have set myself another very important requirement: keep articles concise. People nowadays don’t have time to read, not everything, not with a list of videos to watch as well, with series to follow, video games to complete, social life to fulfill. Myself included.

As for the frequency of posting, I was thinking at least one article a month. I failed for the first month: it was an exceptionally very busy one, and I also needed to fine tune MANY things for this blog. But I’m confident for the next ones…

… unless I decide to enjoy summer a bit harder 🌞😎🍸
